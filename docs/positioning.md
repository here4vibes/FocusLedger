# FocusLedger Positioning

> **A small, closed world for your attention. The app built to be finished.**

This is the reference the product's copy, features, and marketing are held to. When a headline, a Buddy line, a nudge, or a feature decision is in question, it should be answerable from this document.

---

## The problem: the world stopped closing

For almost all of human history we lived in small, local worlds — a village, a trade, a family — and each one *closed* around us. It felt like the only world there was.

The philosopher **Agnes Callard** calls what replaced it the **uni-context**: telecommunications collapsed those many small worlds into one global context that **never closes** — because it feeds a deep human aversion to being shut in. That open-endedness is the attention crisis, the endless comparison, the sense of a self with nowhere to stand.

**ADHD brains — novelty-seeking, exquisitely context-sensitive — suffer it worst.**

Every productivity app is another node in that uni-context: one more infinite surface, tuned to never let you finish.

## The position

**FocusLedger re-closes a small world.** One bounded place where your tasks, money, and attention live — sealed off from the infinite, small enough to finish.

We don't sell more engagement. **We sell an ending.**

## How the product *is* the position

| Surface | The closing move |
| --- | --- |
| **One tab, not a feed** | You live inside *your* ledger — not the world's scroll. |
| **The Daily Three** | Finitude as a feature. A small, closed set — not infinite choice. |
| **Focus Mode** | The world shut out. One task, the door closed behind it. |
| **Done for today** | An explicit end state that *sends you away* — the app is meant to finish. |
| **The money tab** | Impulse triage asks the closing question: *was that yours — or the market's?* |
| **Notifications** | Come do one small thing, then go. Measured by complete-and-leave, never dwell time. |

## The line we're testing

A live landing A/B, with the winner surfaced in the admin dashboard on a real two-proportion significance test:

- **Control · deficit frame** — *"Stop letting yourself down."* Recruits through shame. Works, but sets the wrong relationship with the product.
- **Variant · aspiration frame** — *"Become the person your to-do list keeps promising."* Recruits through identity and hope — the frame the product now actually delivers.

## Voice: the vocabulary of closure

**Say:** finish · close · enough · small · yours · done · quiet · bounded

**Don't say:** more · endless · optimize · crush · hustle · unlimited · maximize · engage

A concrete test for any piece of copy: *does it help the user stop, or pull them to keep going?* If it pulls, rewrite it.

## The synthesis: close a world, and a self can form

You can't become anyone in particular when you're everywhere at once. Callard's other idea — **aspiration**, the slow work of becoming who you're reaching for (*Aspiration: The Agency of Becoming*, 2018) — requires a bounded self with ordered values. The uni-context dissolves exactly that.

So the two ideas are one product: **we draw the boundary inside which a becoming can happen.** That is a sharper reason to exist than "executive function on autopilot" — and it's timely, because this is the conversation the culture is having right now.

### Design & tone implications (working rules)

- **Peak-*end*, not peak-engagement.** Sessions should reach a clear end and release the user. If a change increases dwell time, it is suspect on these terms even if it "works" on engagement.
- **Buddy coaches by asking, not advising** (see the aspiration voice pass): a question that makes the user examine their own life beats a tip.
- **A lapse is the middle of becoming, never a broken streak.** No shame in any surface.
- **Notifications must be world-*closing*.** A nudge returns the user to one small local task, then lets them leave.
